1. The filmmakers discuss their intent to create a film about José Rizal. What challenges do they anticipate in portraying his life?

José Rizal is a national hero in the Philippines, so there would be high expectations for the film to honor his legacy. Filmmakers would need to be sensitive to
the emotional and cultural significance of his life for the Filipino people while still telling a compelling and dramatic story.

2. A debate arises about the authenticity of Rizal's retraction document. What are the main arguments presented regarding its validity?

Some argue that Rizal may have chosen to sign the retraction as a strategic decision to save his family and prevent further bloodshed. Given the oppressive political
environment, his retraction could have been a way to ease his execution and protect the people around him. This would explain why Rizal, under pressure, might have made such a concession.

3. The character of Josephine Bracken is introduced. How does the film depict her relationship with Rizal?

Josephine Bracken is often depicted as a devoted and loving partner to Rizal. Despite the political and social challenges surrounding their relationship,
she is shown as being deeply compassionate and supportive. Their relationship is often depicted as one of genuine affection,
with Josephine standing by Rizal during his imprisonment and final days.

4. The filmmakers interview a historian about Rizal's alleged retraction. What evidence is provided to support or refute the claim?

The historian might point to the testimonies of individuals who were present during Rizal’s final moments, particularly the priests and Spanish authorities.
They claim that Rizal signed the retraction document before his execution. For instance, some Catholic priests,
such as Father Pedro Pio, testified that they witnessed Rizal's act of retraction, asserting that he renounced his writings against the church and colonial authorities.

5. The film presents different portrayals of Rizal’s execution. How does each version differ, and what do these differences suggest?

In this version, Rizal is depicted as a calm, composed, and resolute figure as he faces his execution. He stands as a symbol of unwavering courage and
sacrifice for the cause of freedom. His death is shown as a pivotal moment in Philippine history, emphasizing his
martyrdom for the nation’s independence.

6. One of the characters questions whether Rizal was truly a hero. What reasoning do they give to challenge his heroism?

One argument that challenges Rizal's heroism is his relatively moderate stance on reform compared to more radical revolutionaries like Andres Bonifacio
or Emilio Aguinaldo. Rizal advocated for reforms through education, peaceful means, and dialogue with the Spanish authorities, rather than through violent revolution.

7. A scene features a discussion about Rizal’s religious beliefs. How does the film depict his stance on faith and the Catholic Church?

The film might portray Rizal as a devout Catholic who valued spiritual faith but was deeply critical of the abuses perpetrated by the Spanish clergy. Rizal’s criticisms of the
Church were primarily centered around the way the clergy used religion as a tool of control and oppression, particularly in
the Philippines, where many priests exploited their power over the Filipino people.

8. The film uses a unique narrative style, including fictionalized conversations with Rizal. How does this technique affect storytelling?

The fictionalized conversations give the filmmakers room for creative interpretation of Rizal's character and his life, allowing them to emphasize particular aspects that
align with the film's overall message or vision. This approach allows the filmmakers to blend fact and fiction in a way
that serves the narrative, making the historical events more accessible and engaging.

9. At one point, the filmmakers express frustration with their research on Rizal. What specific issue causes this frustration?

The key frustration the filmmakers express could be related to the question of whether or not Rizal truly retracted his previous criticisms of the Catholic Church and the
Spanish colonial government before his death. This has been a subject of debate for years, with some claiming that
Rizal signed a retraction document, while others argue that it was forged or coerced.

10. The film concludes with a reflection on Rizal's legacy. How do the filmmakers interpret his impact on contemporary Philippine society?

The filmmakers highlight Rizal’s death as a symbol of heroism and self-sacrifice. His martyrdom continues to inspire those fighting for justice and equality. His legacy is
invoked during times of political unrest. Rizal’s example reminds Filipinos to stand up for the oppressed. His death shows the cost
of fighting for a fair society. His story calls modern Filipinos to continue the fight for freedom and human rights.
